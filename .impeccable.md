## Design Context

### Users
Central Bank of Uzbekistan economists, Ministry of Economy & Finance analysts, CERR researchers, international organization country desks (IMF, World Bank, ADB), and university economics departments. They are domain experts who understand macroeconomic models and expect precision. They use this in professional settings — at their desks, preparing policy briefs, running scenario analyses before meetings. They need to trust the output enough to base real decisions on it.

### Brand Personality
**Authoritative. Precise. Institutional.**

This is a serious analytical tool, not a dashboard toy. It should feel like it was built by economists for economists — the kind of tool that earns credibility by how it presents information, not by how it decorates it.

### Emotional Goals
The primary emotion is **confidence** — users should feel that the platform is rigorous, reliable, and professionally built. Every visual choice should reinforce trust: clean data presentation, consistent patterns, no ambiguity in what numbers mean.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Visual tone:** Clean, professional, data-dense. Institutional without being sterile. Editorial rigor — closer to a central bank publication or a Financial Times interactive than a startup dashboard.
- **Light mode only** (no dark mode planned).
- **Positive references:**
  - **Our World in Data** — clarity of explanation, chart-forward layouts, restrained palette, generous type.
  - **FRED (St. Louis Fed)** — utilitarian, no-nonsense data presentation, trust through consistency.
  - **Financial Times** — editorial typography, confident use of color on white, serif/sans pairing, narrative data journalism.
- **Anti-references:** Generic govtech portals, crypto/trading dashboards, SaaS admin templates. No pastel gradients, friendly blobs, oversized icons, infographic filler. No "Claude AI aesthetic."
- **Palette:** Open to evolution beyond the current navy/teal. Consider an FT-adjacent direction — a warmer off-white paper background (not cool slate), a single confident primary (deep navy or ink), and 1–2 meaningful accents rather than the current 7-color rainbow. Reserve color for semantics (gain/loss, shock/baseline), not decoration.
- **Uzbek identity — welcome but subtle.** Rootedness, not ornament. Draw from Registan tilework (deep indigo, ceramic turquoise, terracotta) or ikat geometry as restrained accent choices, never as surface decoration or borders. The platform should feel *of* Uzbekistan without cosplay.
- **Typography — reconsider current choices.** Inter and JetBrains Mono are the two most over-used "default technical" fonts in the AI era and work against an editorial/institutional voice. Prefer:
  - **Body/UI:** a humanist sans with more character than Inter — e.g., *Hanken Grotesk*, *Söhne* (if licensed), *Untitled Sans*, *Source Sans 3*, or *Albert Sans*.
  - **Display/Editorial:** a serious serif for headlines and report titles — e.g., *Source Serif 4*, *Literata*, *Spectral*, or *EB Garamond* — to signal editorial weight the way FT/Economist do.
  - **Tabular data:** a proper tabular monospace like *JetBrains Mono* is acceptable for raw code/JSON, but numeric figures in tables should use tabular-figure OpenType features of the body sans, not a monospace (which feels "developer tool," not "publication").
  - Cyrillic and Latin must both be first-class — verify full script coverage before committing.

### Design Principles

1. **Data first, decoration never.** Every pixel should serve comprehension. No ornamental gradients, illustrations, or visual filler. If an element doesn't help the user understand the economics, remove it.

2. **Consistency builds trust.** Use the same tokens, spacing, and patterns across all 6 models and the hub. Design drift between pages (e.g., different color values for the same semantic role) erodes the sense of a unified, reliable platform.

3. **Density is a feature.** The audience reads spreadsheets and IMF reports daily. Don't over-space or over-simplify. Compact, well-structured information density is a sign of respect for the user's expertise.

4. **Quiet confidence over loud design.** Subtle shadows, restrained color use, precise alignment. The platform should feel like it was built by people who care about getting the economics right — the design reflects that same rigor.

5. **Institutional, not corporate.** Think central bank annual report, not SaaS landing page. Professional gravitas without the glossy marketing energy.

6. **Rooted, not decorated.** Uzbek identity enters through palette nuance and material restraint (ceramic turquoise, indigo, warm paper), never through surface ornament (no ikat borders, no tile patterns as backgrounds, no flag-color banners).

- Model pages use their own independent CSS rather than shared tokens
- No shared CSS file — tokens are duplicated and diverge across pages
