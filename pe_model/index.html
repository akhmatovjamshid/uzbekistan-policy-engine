Analysis Report

Model Overview

The Partial Equilibrium (PE) model estimates the trade impact of tariff changes on Uzbekistan's imports in the chemicals and plastics sector (HS 28–40). The model is based on the WITS-SMART methodology (World Integrated Trade Solution — Software for Market Analysis and Restrictions on Trade), widely used by the World Bank and WTO.

Unlike general equilibrium models, the PE model isolates the direct market-level effects of a tariff change, holding all other markets constant. It is particularly suited for analysing sector-specific trade policy reforms such as WTO accession commitments.

Trade Creation

Trade creation (TC) measures the increase in imports from WTO MFN-tariff countries following a tariff reduction. As tariffs fall, domestic prices decline, stimulating demand for imports from liberalised sources.

TCij = Mij · εd · |Δt/(1 + t)|

where:
Mij = base imports from country j in sector i (USD)
εd = import demand elasticity (default = 1.27)
Δt = change in tariff rate (negative for reductions)
t = current applied tariff rate
Δt/(1+t) = Armington price change factor

Trade Diversion

Trade diversion (TD) captures the shift in import sourcing from FTA/CIS partners (who already face zero tariffs) to newly competitive WTO MFN partners. When MFN rates fall, the relative price advantage enjoyed by FTA partners diminishes, diverting trade toward MFN suppliers.

TDij = MijFTA · εs · |Δt/(1 + t)|

where:
MijFTA = FTA partner imports in the same product
εs = substitution elasticity between sources

Welfare Effects

The model calculates two main welfare components:

Consumer surplus gain: as import prices fall, domestic consumers pay less — equivalent to the area under the demand curve between the old and new price levels.

Government revenue change: lower tariff rates on existing and new imports reduce tariff revenue, which is a transfer from the government budget. The net welfare effect is the consumer surplus gain minus the revenue loss plus any production efficiency gains.

Welfare = Consumer Surplus Gain − Government Revenue Loss

Scenarios

Three WTO accession scenarios are modelled, reflecting possible negotiated tariff bindings relative to the current applied MFN rate:

• Scenario 1 (−20%): Moderate liberalisation — MFN applied rates reduced by 20% of current level

• Scenario 2 (−50%): Substantial liberalisation — MFN rates halved relative to current applied level

• Scenario 3 (−80%): Near-full liberalisation — MFN rates reduced by 80%, approaching the CIS FTA zero-rate levels
